Critical After 6 Months

Iron

Babies are born with iron stores accumulated during the third trimester of pregnancy. Those stores are sufficient for roughly the first four to six months of life. After that, breast milk — which contains only about 0.2 to 0.4 mg of iron per liter — cannot come close to meeting a growing infant's needs. After six months, babies need approximately 11 mg of iron per day. Breast milk delivers a fraction of that. Iron deficiency at this stage is not a minor concern: it directly affects hemoglobin production, brain development, and cognitive function. You cannot reliably close this gap through food alone, especially in the early months of introducing solids.

The problem with most baby iron supplements is tolerability. Ferrous sulfate — the form found in standard pediatric drops — is notorious for causing constipation, dark stools, and a metallic taste that babies fight tooth and nail. Constantly Overlooked

Fish Oil

DHA is the dominant structural fatty acid in the human brain. The brain roughly doubles in size during the first year of life, and DHA is critical for visual development and healthy nervous system formation. Breast milk contains DHA, but the concentration varies significantly based on maternal diet. Even formula-fortified DHA is often in a less absorbable ethyl-ester form rather than the triglyceride form naturally found in fish. The reality is that an estimated 99% of children receive no omega-3 supplementation regularly. This is a gap worth closing.
